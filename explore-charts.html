Learn About This Activity

The bottom of the Great Lakes is the resting place for thousands of ships that have sunk over the last 400 years.
These shipwrecks can be found across all five Great Lakes, including many in eastern Lake Ontario near Kingston.

Each shipwreck is like a time capsule of history from the time the ship was built and sunk.

Most of us will never get to visit these wrecks, but luckily many professional and recreational divers have made the journey and brought back photos.

Madeira 1/2


Three major storms occurred on the Great Lakes in 1905: on September 2nd, October 20th, and November 28th.

The Madeira with sails furled, alongside a smaller steam ship.

Madeira 2/2


Ten ships sank in these storms on Lakes Superior, Huron, and Erie, with many more damaged. The Madeira was being towed by a steamship, but was cut loose in the storm and crashed into a cliff.

Colour photo of the wreck of the Madeira, as viewed from above from a nearby cliff.

Hesper 1/3


Launched in 1890, the Hesper was a wooden-hulled steamer primarily used to transport iron ore and grain.

The Hesper at a dock.

Hesper 2/3


It was caught in a spring storm and foundered on a reef. No lives were lost, but the wreck was considered a total loss.

The Hesper at a dock, viewed from the starboard bow.

Hesper 3/3


The Hesper on the water, viewed in profile.

The Hesper on the water, viewed in profile.

Kamloops 1/2


The SS Kamloops was built specifically for travel within the Great Lakes, designed to fit in the tight confines of the Welland Canal.

The SS Kamloops on the water, viewed from the side.

Kamloops 2/2


As part of the Canada Steamship Lines fleet, it delivered cargo between ports on the Great Lakes and St. Lawrence River. It foundered in a winter storm, and remained undiscovered for 50 years.

The SS Kamloops on the water, viewed from the side.

Monarch 1/2


The Monarch foundered when it crashed headlong into a solid rock wall on the tip of Isle Royale during a snowstorm on its last trip of the season.


The SS Monarch on the water. Black smoke billows from the ship’s stack.

Monarch 2/2


All men but one managed to make it to shore, where they survived for four days while awaiting rescue.

The wreck of the SS Monarch. Ropes, gears, and loose timbers are visible alongside a diver.

Alvin Clark 1/2


The Alvin Clark was raised intact in 1969, and set up as a tourist attraction in a nearby town.

The Alvin Clark at a dock, after it was raised. Viewed from just off the bow of the ship.

Alvin Clark 2/2


However, no preservation measures were put in place, and the ship and its contents rapidly deteriorated once exposed to air. Its remains were demolished in 1994. The story of the Alvin Clark illustrates the importance of preservation and conservation of shipwrecks.

Monochromatic photo of the Alvin Clark at a dock after it was raised. Several people stand on the dock and look at the ship.

Frank O’Connor 1/2


The Frank O’Connor was one of the largest wooden ships to ever travel on the Great Lakes, at 301 feet (92 metres) long.

The propellor of the wreck of the Frank O’Connor.

Frank O’Connor 2/2


The hull was strengthened with iron and steel strapping, and the ship was powered by a modern steam engine. It caught fire on its way to Milwaukee, WI, and though the ship sank, all crew members survived.

The Frank O’Connor as it exits a lock.

Carl D. Bradley 1/2


The Carl D. Bradley was headed for winter dry dock maintenance when it was caught in a violent storm.

Aerial view of the Carl D. Bradley as it moves forward on the water.

Carl D. Bradley 2/2


The ship broke in two, and all but two of its crew perished. It is the second-longest shipwreck in the Great Lakes, at 639 feet (195 metres), after the SS Edmund Fitzgerald.

The self-unloader of the Carl D. Bradley as it empties its cargo.

Daniel J. Morrell 1/4


A freighter that had been used on the Great Lakes for 60 years, the Daniel J. Morrell got caught in a violent storm and broke in two pieces.

The Daniel J. Morrell on water.

Daniel J. Morrell 2/4


As the bow sank, the stern continued steaming before sinking five miles away. Only one person in the 29-man crew survived.

The Daniel J. Morrell on calm water. Black smoke billows from the ship’s stack.

Charles S. Price 1/2


The Charles S. Price was one of eight ships lost with all hands in lower Lake Huron in the infamous Great Storm of 1913.

A small portion of an overturned hull that extends out of the water.

Charles S. Price 2/2


This storm, which ravaged Lakes Superior, Michigan, Huron, and Erie, lasted three days, from November 8-10. In total, 67 ships were lost or damaged, and at least 248 lives lost.

The Charles S. Price on the water, viewed from the side.

Waubuno 1/2


The Waubuno was carrying a heavy load of freight and passengers when it was caught in a storm and foundered.

The Waubuno viewed from the side.

Waubuno 2/2


Approximately 40 people were on board, all of whom perished. The wreck, located in shallow water in eastern Georgian Bay, is a popular dive site.

The Waubuno, docked and viewed from the side. Men stand along a rail on the ship.

Argo


The Argo was carrying crude oil and benzol when it foundered in a storm. In 2015, 78 years after it sank, it was found to be leaking hazardous chemicals into Lake Erie. A cleanup response was completed, removing all of the remaining chemicals from the wreck.

Monochromatic side-scan sonar image of the wreck of the Argo on the lake bottom, viewed from above.

Admiral


The Admiral was towing a tanker barge, the Cleveco, from Toledo to Cleveland when it encountered a winter storm. The Admiral foundered and the Cleveco was left without propulsion. Rescue ships were unable to locate the Cleveco; it is believed to have sunk while awaiting assistance.

Black and white photo of the steamship Admiral. Several people stand on the deck.

James B. Colgate 1/3


Whaleback steamers have hulls that curve above the waterline, designed to reduce the impact of waves on the sides of the ship.

The ship whaleback steamer James B. Colgate on water.

James B. Colgate 2/3


On October 20, 1916, a massive storm swept over Lake Erie, sinking four ships including the James B. Colgate and killing 49 people. It later became known as Black Friday.

The ship whaleback steamer James B. Colgate on water.

James B. Colgate 3/3

G P Griffith 1/2


The G P Griffith was carrying 326 passengers when the ship caught fire in the night and struck a sandbar while attempting to get to shore.

Etching of the “SS G. P. Griffith”

G P Griffith 2/2


Though it was only half a mile from shore, most passengers could not swim and subsequently drowned. Death toll estimates range from 250 to 295 people, making this the third-greatest loss of life in any Great Lakes shipping disaster.

Photo of “SS G. P. Griffith” historical marker.

HMS Ontario 1/2


During the Revolutionary War, HMS Ontario was the largest British warship to sail on the Great Lakes.

The bow of the wreck HMS Ontario.

HMS Ontario 2/2


It was launched just five months before it sank, and was used to ferry troops, supplies, and prisoners. It never saw battle.

The stern of the wreck HMS Ontario.

Milan 1/2


Built in 1845, the Milan sprung a leak while on its way from Oswego, NY to Cleveland, OH with a cargo of 1000 barrels of salt.

The wreck of the Milan, showing the deck near the bow from above. One mast still stands upright.

Milan 2/2


The crew tried unsuccessfully to pump out the inflowing water and make it to shore, but eventually had to abandon ship. Nine crew members and a Newfoundland dog were on board the Milan, all of whom were safely rescued by a passing ship.

The bow of the wreck of the Milan.

Three-Brothers 1/4


Dagger-board schooners were used in the Great Lakes in the early 19th century.

Painting of the wreck of the Three Brothers.

Three-Brothers 2/4


A board extending through the hull acted as a keel in rough waters, but could be removed to bring the ship close to shore.

The wreck of the Three Brothers.

Three-Brothers 3/4


Three Brothers was the first fully-functional ship of this type ever found.

The bow of the wreck of the Three Brothers.

Roberval 1/2


The Roberval made frequent trips between Ottawa, ON and Oswego, NY in the early 20th century to exchange coal and lumber.

The Roberval billowing black smoke on the water while in service, circa early 1900s.

Roberval 2/2


The large load of spruce it was carrying when it sank saved four people, as they managed to stay afloat on a raft of lumber until help arrived.

Painting of The Roberval.

KPH 1/2


The KPH wreck, named for its proximity to the Kingston Psychiatric Hospital, is a well-known dive site in Kingston.

The ship Waterlily on the water with a full load of barrels on the deck and several men standing aboard.

KPH 2/2


It is believed that the wreck may be the remains of the Waterlily, a steam barge that was constructed in 1870 and operated until at least 1916.

The bow of the KPH wreck on the lake bottom.

HMS St. Lawrence 1/2


The HMS St. Lawrence is considered the largest sailing war vessel ever to sail the Great Lakes.

Detail of the wreck of the HMS St. Lawrence. Algae grows on the few remaining planks of wood of the hull.

HMS St. Lawrence 2/2


Launched in September 1814 for use in the War of 1812, the ship carried 102 guns and could support almost 1,000 men. The St. Lawrence never saw battle.

The wreck of the HMS St. Lawrence. Aquatic plants grow between parallel wooden boards.

HMS Prince Regent 1/2


The HMS Prince Regent was a British warship that patrolled Lake Ontario during the War of 1812.

The remains of the hull of the HMS Prince Regent. Wooden planks run in parallel, most of which have zebra mussels on them.

HMS Prince Regent 2/2


It was built in Kingston at the Royal Naval Dockyards, where it was launched in April 1814. It had little use after the war, and was put up for sale in the early 1830s, but no buyer was found and it eventually sank in Deadman Bay.

The remains of the hull of the HMS Prince Regent. Wooden planks are covered in algae and zebra mussels.

Wolfe Islander II 1/2


After almost 30 years ferrying vehicles between Kingston and Wolfe Island, the Wolfe Islander II was scuttled to create a dive site, the first such use of a commercial vessel in the Great Lakes.

The Wolfe Islander II on the water as it ferries cars.

Wolfe Islander II 2/2


Since then, it has become the most-visited shipwreck in Lake Ontario.

Two divers on the wreck of the Wolfe Islander II, in the middle of the ship. A motorcycle stands on the deck below the divers.

Horace Taber


Constructed in St. Clair, MI in 1867, the Horace Taber became a Canadian vessel in 1911. It was headed to Kingston with a load of coal in late 1922 when it got caught in a snow storm and ran ashore. Though the ship was a complete wreck, the cargo was salvaged.

A three-masted schooner at a dock in Kingston.

Effie Mae 1/2


From ca. 1980 to 1992, the Effie Mae was used as a live-aboard charter ship for scuba diving.

A diver in the wreck of the Effie Mae. The diver’s upper body sticks out of an open door or window in the wreck.

Effie Mae 2/2


In the early-1990s, the ship’s owners were moving out of Ontario and donated the vessel to Preserve Our Wrecks Kingston. The ship was scuttled next to the schooner-barge Aloha in approximately 17 metres of water, and remains a popular dive site.

A three-masted schooner at a dock in Kingston.

Aloha 1/2


Built in 1888, the Aloha was being towed to Kingston with a load of 925 tons of coal when it was caught in a storm and foundered.

The wreck of the Aloha, with the ship's boiler in the foreground and a diver beside it.

Aloha 2/2


The tug towing the ship, the Chamberlain, managed to save some of the crew, though the captain of the Aloha drowned.

Detail of a diver and the bow of the Aloha. The bow is mostly covered by zebra mussels, though roman numerals are visible in some places underneath.

Comet 1/2


Built in 1848, the Comet foundered and was raised and repaired several times in its life.

The wreck of the Comet, viewed from mid-ship to highlight two large paddlewheels on the sides of the ship.

Comet 2/2


It struck a shoal in its first few trips and sank, then in 1851 its boiler exploded, tearing a hole in its hull and sinking again. After several more accidents, it was caught in a storm and struck another ship. Taking on water, passengers and crew abandoned ship, leaving the Comet to sink for the last time.

The wreck of the Cornwall. A large paddlewheel is visible in the foreground, while a diver examines it in the background.

George T. Davie 1/2


Built in 1898, the George T. Davie spent 47 years hauling coal and grain on Lake Ontario and the St. Lawrence River.

A diver shines a flashlight on the wheel of the wreck of the George T. Davie.

George T. Davie 2/2


On a routine trip delivering coal from Oswego, NY to Kingston, the ship began leaking and sank. All crew members were picked up by the tug Salvage Prince, which had been towing the ship.

A diver above the remains of a lifeboat. The hull of the George T. Davie sits in the background.
