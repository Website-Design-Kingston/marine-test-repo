THE USE OF PHOTOGRAPHY


The digital camera is a marine archaeologist's best friend.

Not only do photographs capture a wreck in amazing detail, but digital photos can be used in a technique called photogrammetry to create an accurate 3D model of the wreck-site.

Photogrammetry requires an archeologist to take hundreds of overlapping photos over every part of a wreck.

The photos are analyzed by a computer to build highly accurate model of the wreck that can be studied out of water.

SHIP PLANS


These drawings are the ship plans drafted just after the War of 1812 to record how HMS St. Lawrence was built. The ship was actually built so quickly during the war that the plans were drawn after the ship was launched and the war was over!

HMS St. Lawrence had three tiers of guns-decks carrying as many as 56 guns per side.

Compared to ocean going warships, Great Lakes ships tended to be shallow-drafted. They were never long at sea, so they could be designed to maximize the number of guns they could carry (guns are what we think of as canons!).

Archaeologists can compare these original plans to the actual remains of the shipwreck to figure out what part of the ship we see on the lake bottom today.

Strickland, Thomas. HMS Saint Lawrence (1814). May 1815. Paper, black ink, red ink, green ink. National Marine Museum, UK.

A NEW PURPOSE


In 1833, the hulk of HMS St. Lawrence was towed out of Navy Bay by a side-wheel steamship, across town to the site of the Morton Brewery and Distillery.

Story has it that nearing its destination, high winds ran the ship into the land just offshore of the brewery.

Here, the mighty hulk would serve as a supply dock where steamships could pull alongside and load up with a wood. HMS St. Lawrence would never again move from this spot.

Overtime, the ship was likely salvaged for wood, as it slowly decayed into the water, leaving only the remains we see today.

POST-WAR DECAY


This painting, by artist James Gray shows the covered hulks of HMS St. Lawrence and other decommissioned warships that were fixtures of Kingston's waterfront for many years.

HMS St. Lawrence is the large hull near the left side of the painting.

When the War of 1812 ended, HMS St. Lawrence was laid up at the Royal Naval Dockyards (near the current site of Kingston's Royal Military College).

All its guns and masts were removed. Only its hulk remained, slowly decaying.

Gray, James. Kingston from Fort Henery. 1828. Aquatint, etching and water color on wove paper. Baldwin Collection, Toronto Reference Library.

ARCHAEOLOGY IN 3D


These 3D images show what remains of HMS St. Lawrence today. An accurate 3D model of the wreck was created using photogrammetry, a technique that processes many hundreds of photographs to recreate the wreck-site in three dimensions.

Although only a 30-metre section of the lower hull remains, photogrammetry allows marine archaeologists to closely compare the remains to the original ship drawings, in order to identify the ship and learn about how it was built.

This particular model was created by analyzing nearly 1,600 photographs of the wreck.

A 3D rendering created with photogrammetry showing the remains of the keel section of the HMS St. Lawrence. The model was based on approximately 1,600 photographs taken by Jonathan Moore, a marine archeologist from Parks Canada.

A GREAT WARSHIP


This is a painting from around 1815 (the artist is unknown), depicting the launch of HMS St. Lawrence. It was large as some of the ocean-going warships of the time.

To this day, it is the largest sailing ship ever launched on the Great Lakes. It had three gun-decks, 58 metres in length, and could carry up to 112 guns (which we sometimes call canons).

For the remainder of the war, no American ship dared confront this mighty ship, so just the fact that HMS St. Lawrence even existed, gave the British complete control of Lake Ontario.

And so it was that the mightiest warship of the Great Lakes never engaged in actual combat!

Artist Unknown, “The Launching of the St. Lawrence, 112 Guns, 1815”. Pen, ink, watercolour. Royal Ontario Museum (ROM Accession 967.106.1).

SOLD AT AUCTION


After the war of 1812, British and American warships were no longer needed, or even allowed to sail the Great Lakes.

For 17 years the hulk of the mighty HMS St. Lawrence was moored in the Royal Navy Dockyard. Its masts and rigging were removed along with all its guns, and the hull was covered to protect it from the elements.

Finally, late in 1831, The Royal Navy decided it was time to get rid of all its unused warships. So the HMS St. Lawrence and three other warships were put up for public auction, as shown in the newspaper clipping to the right.

Early the following year, HMS St. Lawrence was sold to the Morton Brewery & Distillery for £25, which is less than $3,000 dollars in today’s money. In 1833, the hulk of the HMS St. Lawrence was towed to its new home across town.

His Majesty’s Dock Yard, Kingston, U.C, 15th November, 1831.

NOTICE is hereby given, that on the 18th day of January, 2832, at 10 o’clock, A.M. the Hulls of the following Ships as they now law on the shore off the King’s Dock Yard at Kingston, Upper Canada, will be disposed of by Public Auction, viz : St. Lawrence of 112 Guns. Kingston of 56 Guns. Burlington of 42 Guns. Montreal of 24 Guns. Also, the Frames of the Wolf and Canada, each pierced for 110 guns, as they stand on the Stocks in Dock Yard.

Immediately after the above Sale, a quantity of standing and running Rigging, late belonging to the St. Lawrence, Kingston, Burlington and Montreal, will in like manner be disposed of. As this Rigging has been but little used, and is in good condition, it may be advantageously related or otherwise converted.

The Ships will be disposed of separately, and the Rigging will be divided into lots to suit purchasers. A reasonable time will be allowed for the removal of the articles from the King’s premises, which must be done at the purchaser’s expense. A deposit of twenty-five per cent will be required at the time of sale, and the remainder of the purchase money previous to the removal of the articles.

Further particulars may be learned at the Naval Store Keeper’s Office, at Kingston Dock Yard, and of Mr. William Lunn, clerk in charge of naval stores at Montreal. On the opening of the navigation in 1832, about Twelve Hundred Spurs for Masts and Yards, of from four to thirty—four inches in diameter, will be disposed of by Public Auction. Timely notice will be given of the days of sale. The spars may be previously examined; for which purpose application must be made at the Naval Store Keeper’s Office. J.R. GLOVER, Naval Store Keeper.
